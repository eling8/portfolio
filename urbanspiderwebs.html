Urban Spiderwebs: Detecting and Modeling Traffic Congestion within Urban Road Networks

Using network analysis, graph centrality measures, and traffic analysis to identify potential areas of high traffic congestion in cities around the world.

As city populations continue to swell, city planners and civil engineers need to match the tide of incoming people with scalable transportation systems that can support a growing population, or else face gridlock congestion and immobility undesirable for inhabitants and visitors.

In our project, we used various measures of graph centrality including betweenness, approximate weighted betweenness, and closeness centrality, as well as a custom metric of urbanness, to identify which areas of a city's road network has the greatest potential for traffic congestion for vehicles. We created traffic simulations for dynamic, empirical insight and visualizations into which streets experience the most vehicle traffic. After drawing conclusions from our results, we compared and contrasted the different metrics of traffic congestion, as well as the differences observed in cities across the world and their points of peak traffic.
